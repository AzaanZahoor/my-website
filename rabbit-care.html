Rabbit Care: Feeding, Housing, Handling, Grooming, Behaviour, Diseases & Prevention

For beginners, pet owners & small farmers • Reading time: 10–14 minutes
Small animals Home care Simple English

Rabbits are gentle, intelligent and sensitive animals. They bond easily with owners and can live 8–12 years with proper care. However, rabbits require specific food, safe housing, regular grooming and careful handling. Wrong feeding or stressful environment can cause serious health problems.

This beginner-friendly guide explains everything you need to know: feeding, housing, behaviour, grooming, diseases, handling and daily care—written in simple English.

1. Understanding rabbit behaviour

Rabbits communicate with body posture, ears and sounds. Knowing their behaviour helps you care better and prevent stress.

Signs of a happy rabbit

  • Relaxed body and half-closed eyes
  • Gentle grinding of teeth (contentment)
  • Binkying (jumping happily)
  • Curiosity and exploring behaviour
  • Normal eating and grooming

Signs of stress or fear

  • Thumping hind legs
  • Hiding all day
  • Rapid breathing
  • Growling or biting
  • Loss of appetite

2. Housing: giving rabbits a safe and clean space

Rabbits need a clean, spacious and well-ventilated area. Poor housing leads to infections, stress and injuries.

Housing requirements

  • A large cage or enclosure where the rabbit can stretch and stand
  • Soft bedding like hay, straw or wood shavings
  • Protection from rain, heat and cold
  • Separate feeding and resting area
  • Clean, dry flooring (avoid wire floors)

Cleanliness

  • Remove droppings daily
  • Change bedding every 2–3 days
  • Wash food and water bowls daily
  • Keep cage away from predators (cats, dogs, snakes)

3. Feeding rabbits the right way

Diet is the most important part of rabbit care. The wrong diet can cause digestive blockage, obesity and dental disease.

Main foods rabbits need

  • Hay (70% of diet): Must be available 24/7. Essential for digestion and dental health.
  • Fresh vegetables: Carrot tops, spinach, coriander, lettuce, cabbage (small amounts).
  • Pellets: High-quality rabbit pellets in limited amounts.
  • Fresh water: Always available.

Foods rabbits must avoid

  • Chocolate (toxic)
  • Biscuits, bread and sweets
  • Onions and garlic
  • Potatoes
  • Excess fruits (too much sugar)

Feeding schedule

  • Unlimited hay
  • Pellets once or twice a day
  • Vegetables in small, varied amounts
  • Occasional fruit as treat

4. Safe handling of rabbits

Rabbits have delicate bones. Wrong handling can cause injury. Always be gentle.

Correct ways to pick a rabbit

  • Support the chest with one hand
  • Support the back legs with the other hand
  • Hold the rabbit close to your body

Never do this

  • Never pick a rabbit by ears
  • Never lift by scruff of the neck
  • Never chase a rabbit—it causes panic

5. Grooming and hygiene

Rabbits groom themselves, but regular care is important to prevent hairballs, parasites and skin problems.

Grooming routine

  • Brush long-haired rabbits daily
  • Check ears weekly
  • Clean eyes gently if needed
  • Trim nails regularly
  • Do not bathe rabbits unless necessary

Common grooming issues

  • Hairballs
  • Matted fur
  • Dirty bottoms (sign of digestive issue)
  • Fleas or mites

6. Common rabbit diseases and symptoms

Rabbits hide illness, so early observation is extremely important.

Common diseases

  • Diarrhoea or digestive blockage
  • Respiratory infections
  • Ear mites
  • Overgrown teeth
  • Heatstroke
  • Skin infections

Warning signs requiring vet attention

  • No eating or droppings for 12 hours
  • Limping or difficulty moving
  • Runny nose or watery eyes
  • Swollen belly or painful abdomen
  • Rapid breathing or wheezing
  • Overgrown front teeth

Do not give human medicines to rabbits—they can be toxic.

7. Prevention and daily care routine

  • Keep hay available all the time
  • Provide fresh drinking water daily
  • Clean cage regularly
  • Protect from extreme heat
  • Give chew toys to prevent dental issues
  • Handle gently to avoid stress
  • Check droppings daily

8. Summary

Rabbit care is simple when you follow a routine: fresh hay, clean housing, safe handling, grooming and regular observation. Rabbits are sensitive animals, but with gentle treatment and proper feeding, they live healthy, happy and stress-free lives.
