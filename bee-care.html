Bee Care: Hive Management, Feeding & Disease Prevention

For beginners and beekeepers • Reading time: 10–12 minutes
Insects Farm care Simple English

Bees are vital for pollination and honey production. Proper care ensures healthy colonies, high honey yield, and prevents disease outbreaks.

This guide covers hive management, feeding, behaviour, common diseases, and preventive measures for beginner-friendly beekeeping.

1. Understanding bee behaviour

Bees are social insects living in highly organized colonies. Observing their behaviour helps detect problems early.

Signs of healthy bees

  • Active foragers returning with pollen
  • Calm and organized movement inside the hive
  • Regular brood patterns and healthy queen presence
  • Honey and pollen stores increasing steadily

Signs of stress or colony issues

  • Reduced foraging or aggressive behaviour
  • Queenless colony or missing brood
  • Pest infestation: mites, wax moths, ants
  • Foul smell, mold, or unusual hive moisture

2. Hive management

Proper hive management ensures colony growth and honey production.

Key points

  • Use clean, sturdy hives with proper ventilation
  • Inspect hives weekly for pests and disease
  • Remove old or damaged combs
  • Maintain adequate space for brood and honey storage
  • Keep hives in sunny locations with some shade

3. Feeding bees

Feeding is important when natural nectar is insufficient or during winter.

Feed options

  • Sugar syrup (1:1 or 2:1 ratio water to sugar depending on season)
  • Pollen substitute if natural pollen is scarce
  • Provide clean water near the hive

Feeding tips

  • Feed in moderation to avoid robbing by other bees
  • Check that feeders are clean and free from mold
  • Stop supplemental feeding once natural forage is sufficient

4. Common bee diseases

Healthy colonies require disease prevention and early detection.

Common problems

  • Varroa mites – weaken the colony and spread viruses
  • Wax moths – destroy comb and honey stores
  • Foulbrood – bacterial disease affecting larvae
  • Chalkbrood – fungal infection in brood cells

Preventive measures

  • Regular hive inspections and hygiene
  • Remove infected combs and dead colonies
  • Use mite treatments as recommended by experts
  • Keep apiary ventilated and dry

5. Safety and handling

  • Wear protective gear: gloves, veil, and suit
  • Smoke bees gently to calm them before inspection
  • Avoid sudden movements and loud noises near hives
  • Maintain a safe distance from hives for untrained visitors

6. Daily bee care routine

  • Check honey and pollen stores
  • Inspect brood frames for queen and larvae health
  • Observe bee activity outside the hive
  • Maintain cleanliness around hives
  • Record feeding, inspections, and any health issues

7. Summary

Bee care involves proper hive management, feeding, disease prevention, and careful observation. Following these steps ensures strong, healthy colonies with consistent honey production.
