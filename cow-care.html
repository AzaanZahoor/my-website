Cow Care: Feeding, Behaviour, Common Diseases & Basic Treatment

Written for beginners and caretakers • Approximate reading time: 10–12 minutes
Farm animals Welfare focused Simple English

Cows are gentle, social and intelligent farm animals. Taking care of them is a daily responsibility that directly affects milk quality, fertility, behaviour and overall health. Whether you manage a single cow or a full herd, the basic rules of care remain the same: clean housing, balanced feeding, regular health checks and calm handling.

Remember: Healthy cows give healthy milk. Good management prevents most diseases and reduces veterinary costs.

1. Understanding basic cow behaviour

A cow’s behaviour is one of the earliest indicators of health. Cows follow routines—they like to eat, rest and ruminate at predictable times. Any sudden change in behaviour should be taken seriously.

Signs of a relaxed cow

  • Regular chewing (rumination) while sitting or standing
  • Calm, steady walking without limping
  • Bright eyes and moist nose
  • Normal interest in feed and water
  • Resting comfortably on one side

Warning signs of stress or discomfort

  • Refusal to eat or drink
  • Lameness or difficulty standing
  • Separation from herd and unusual isolation
  • Rapid breathing or excessive drooling
  • Constant mooing, tail flicking or kicking the sides

Early detection helps prevent bigger issues like mastitis, fever, foot rot or digestive disorders.

2. Housing and environment

Good housing ensures comfort, safety and hygiene. A cow should always have a clean, dry and comfortable resting place with enough space to move freely.

Important housing requirements

  • Ventilation: Ensure fresh air flow to reduce heat stress and ammonia smell.
  • Flooring: Clean, non-slippery and slightly soft flooring prevents injuries.
  • Bedding: Dry straw or sand bedding keeps cows comfortable and reduces mastitis.
  • Space: Provide enough room to lie down, stand up and turn without slipping.
  • Cleanliness: Remove dung at least twice daily to prevent flies and diseases.

3. Feeding and watering cows

A cow’s diet affects milk yield, digestion, fertility and immunity. Feeding must be consistent, clean and appropriate for the cow’s age and purpose (dairy, pregnant, dry cow, calf mother etc.).

General feeding rules

  • Provide green fodder (grass, legumes) daily.
  • Give dry fodder like hay for extra fibre.
  • Use clean, fresh feed—no mould or fungus.
  • Do not change feed suddenly; introduce new feed gradually.
  • Provide access to a mineral mixture or salt lick.

Concentrates and supplements

  • Dairy cows need concentrate feed for milk production.
  • Pregnant cows require extra minerals for healthy calves.
  • Always follow the advice of a veterinarian or nutritionist.

Water needs

A cow drinks 40–80 litres of water per day depending on weather and milk production. Water must always be fresh and easily available.

  • Provide clean water 24/7.
  • Wash troughs daily to prevent algae and worms.
  • During summer, provide water more frequently.

4. Common diseases and health problems

Mastitis

Mastitis is an udder infection caused by bacteria. It results in swollen udders, fever and reduced milk quality.

  • Check for clots or blood in milk.
  • Keep udders clean before and after milking.
  • Use proper milking hygiene to prevent infection.

Foot and hoof problems

Cracks, foot rot and lameness are common in cows kept on wet or dirty floors.

  • Trim hooves regularly.
  • Keep flooring dry and clean.
  • Check for wounds, swelling or foul smell.

Digestive disorders

Bloat, acidosis, indigestion and diarrhoea can occur due to poor-quality or sudden feed changes.

Parasites

Internal and external parasites cause weight loss, dull coat and reduced milk. Deworming and clean housing help prevent this.

5. Prevention and basic treatment steps

Most health issues are preventable through daily monitoring and early action.

Daily health checklist

  • Observe appetite and water intake.
  • Check dung consistency.
  • Examine udders for heat or swelling.
  • Watch for limping or abnormal posture.
  • Keep a record of milk yield, changes and medicines.

When to call the vet immediately

  • Sudden drop in milk production.
  • High fever or rapid breathing.
  • Swollen udder or blood in milk.
  • Severe lameness or inability to stand.
  • No eating or drinking for more than one meal.

Never give human medicines or antibiotics without veterinary consultation. Incorrect dosage can harm the cow or spoil milk for human consumption.

6. Gentle handling and caretaker safety

Cows respond well to calm handling. Rough treatment increases stress and reduces milk production.

  • Approach cows slowly and speak softly.
  • Do not shout or hit the animal.
  • Keep the milking area clean and slip-free.
  • Use proper restraints only when necessary.

7. Summary

Cow care is a daily commitment. With balanced feeding, clean housing, gentle handling and regular health checks, any caretaker can maintain high-quality milk production and ensure a long, healthy life for the cow.
