Camel Care: Feeding, Behaviour, Housing, Health, Diseases & Basic Treatment

Written for farmers, herders & beginners • Approx. reading time: 10–12 minutes
Desert animals Working animals Simple English

Camels are strong, intelligent and highly adaptable animals. They survive in harsh desert climates, carry heavy loads and provide milk, meat and transportation. Even though camels tolerate extreme heat and long walking distances, they still require proper care, balanced feeding, regular health checks and gentle handling to stay healthy and cooperative.

This guide explains camel behaviour, feeding, housing, common diseases, prevention and general management in simple language suitable for beginners.

1. Understanding camel behaviour

Camels communicate through body posture, facial expressions and sounds. A calm camel is easy to handle, while a frightened or stressed camel may spit, kick or refuse to move.

Signs of a healthy, relaxed camel

  • Slow chewing and normal appetite
  • Steady walking and balanced body posture
  • Calm eyes, ears moving normally
  • Standing or sitting comfortably for rest
  • Good interaction with herd members

Signs of stress or illness

  • Refusing food or water
  • Restless movement or lying down excessively
  • Continuous grunting or unusual sounds
  • Nasal discharge, coughing or breathing difficulty
  • Swollen joints, limping or foot pain
  • Sudden aggression or biting

2. Housing and shelter

Although camels tolerate heat, they still need clean and safe shelter to protect them from extreme sun, cold nights, rain and strong winds.

Basic housing requirements

  • Shade: Trees or strong roof for protection from sun.
  • Space: The camel must sit, stand and turn easily.
  • Soft flooring: Sand or dry soil is preferred.
  • Keep the shelter dry to prevent foot diseases.
  • Clean dung and urine daily.
  • Provide a separate area for milking female camels.

Fencing

  • Use sturdy fencing that camels cannot bend or break.
  • Avoid sharp metal edges.

3. Feeding and watering camels

Camels can survive on dry and coarse plants, but for best health they need a balanced diet including roughage, minerals and clean water.

What camels eat

  • Dry grass and hay
  • Leaves of desert shrubs
  • Tree branches (acacia, khejri, etc.)
  • Green fodder (seasonal)
  • Grains in small amounts for working camels

Feeding guidelines

  • Feed 2–3 times a day instead of one big meal.
  • Avoid sudden changes in feed.
  • Provide salt licks and mineral mixture.
  • Do not overfeed grains – it causes digestive issues.
  • Pregnant and lactating camels need extra nutrition.

Water needs

Although camels can tolerate dehydration, they must be offered clean water every day for good health, milk production and stamina.

  • Provide water at least 1–2 times a day.
  • During hot weather, allow frequent drinking.
  • Clean water tanks regularly.

4. Common diseases and health problems

Camels are hardy animals but they can still suffer from respiratory, digestive, parasitic and foot-related problems.

Respiratory infections

Caused by dust, poor shelter or sudden weather changes. Signs include coughing, fever and nasal discharge.

Digestive disorders

Constipation, bloat or diarrhoea due to poor-quality fodder or sudden diet change.

Foot problems

Cracks, swelling or infection due to hard stony ground or dirty floors.

Skin infections

Mange, ticks and fungal issues commonly affect camels.

Parasites

Internal worms cause weight loss and weakness. Deworming is essential every few months.

5. Prevention and basic treatment

Daily health routine

  • Check appetite and drinking behaviour.
  • Observe walking, sitting and overall movement.
  • Examine skin for wounds or parasites.
  • Clean the shelter daily.
  • Record feed, work load and health changes.

When to call a veterinarian

  • Refusal to eat or drink
  • Lameness or swollen joints
  • Continuous diarrhoea or severe constipation
  • High fever or heavy breathing
  • Deep wounds or bleeding
  • Sudden behaviour change

Never give strong human medicines to camels without a vet’s advice. Wrong drugs can damage the liver, stomach or kidneys.

6. Gentle handling and safety

Calm handling builds trust and prevents accidents. Camels remember kind and gentle treatment.

  • Use soft ropes and avoid nose injuries.
  • Do not overload working camels.
  • Handle young camels with patience.
  • Keep distance from back legs to avoid kicks.
  • Never shout or beat the animal.

7. Summary

Good camel care includes balanced feeding, clean shelter, gentle handling, regular health checks and early disease prevention. With proper management, camels remain strong, productive and calm, providing years of service to farmers and herders.
