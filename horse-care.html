Horse Care: Feeding, Behaviour, Grooming, Training, Shelter, Diseases & Basic Treatment

Designed for beginners, riders & stable owners • Reading time: 10–14 minutes
Farm animals Working animals Simple English

Horses are intelligent, social, powerful animals that have been companions of humans for thousands of years. Whether used for riding, farming, sports, or simply for companionship, horses need systematic care to stay healthy and calm.

This guide covers everything about horse care—behaviour, feeding, hoof care, grooming, training basics, common diseases, prevention and stable management in clear, simple English.

1. Understanding horse behaviour

Horses communicate through body language. A calm, confident horse is easy to train, while a stressed horse can become restless or aggressive.

Healthy behaviour signs

  • Active, alert ears that rotate toward sounds
  • Slow, steady breathing
  • Good appetite and chewing fodder peacefully
  • Calm eyes, smooth movement
  • Comfortable interaction with humans and other horses

Stress or illness signs

  • Tail swishing repeatedly
  • Stomping feet or refusing to walk
  • Head shaking or restless pacing
  • Loss of appetite
  • Heavy breathing or nasal discharge
  • Isolation from herd

2. Shelter and environment

Horses need a clean, dry and spacious shelter. Good stables prevent injuries and keep horses protected from extreme weather.

Stable requirements

  • Ventilation: Fresh air reduces respiratory problems.
  • Dry flooring: Wet floors cause hoof infections.
  • Soft bedding (straw, shavings, sand)
  • Regular cleaning to avoid ammonia smell
  • Secure fencing around outdoor paddocks

Daily stable routine

  • Remove manure twice a day
  • Replace wet bedding
  • Clean water buckets
  • Check walls, gates and fencing for safety
  • Ensure airflow without strong drafts

3. Feeding and watering horses

What horses eat

Horses are herbivores and thrive on fibre-rich food. Their digestion works best when they eat small amounts many times a day.

  • Grass — main natural food
  • Hay — provides fibre and energy
  • Grains (oats, barley) — only in limited quantity
  • Mineral mixture and salt lick
  • Fresh vegetables (carrots, beetroot) as treats

Feeding tips

  • Never feed mouldy hay.
  • Divide meals into 3–4 parts.
  • Do not ride immediately after feeding.
  • Increase feed during winter.
  • Pregnant mares need extra nutrition.

Water requirements

Horses drink 25–40 litres of clean water per day depending on weather and workload.

4. Grooming the horse

Grooming is essential not just for appearance but also for skin health, bonding and early detection of injuries.

  • Brush body daily to remove dirt and loose hair
  • Clean hooves with a hoof pick
  • Comb mane and tail to avoid tangles
  • Check for cuts, ticks or swelling
  • Bath only when necessary to avoid skin dryness

5. Basic training for horses

Horses respond to calm, consistent training. Never punish harshly—fear makes training more difficult.

  • Teach walking, stopping and turning gently
  • Use voice commands with hand signals
  • Reward good behaviour with pats or treats
  • Regular short sessions work best
  • Always use proper riding gear

6. Hoof care

Healthy hooves are essential because a horse stands and works on them all day. Poor hoof care leads to lameness and infections.

  • Clean hooves daily
  • Trim every 4–6 weeks
  • Avoid wet or muddy ground
  • Call a farrier for shoeing if needed

7. Common diseases in horses

Colic

The most serious digestive problem. Signs include rolling, pawing, refusing food, looking at belly. This requires urgent veterinary care.

Laminitis

Painful hoof inflammation caused by overeating grains or sudden diet change.

Respiratory infections

Coughing, nasal discharge, fever—often due to dusty bedding or poor ventilation.

Skin infections

Caused by parasites, dirty bedding or excessive moisture.

Worm infestation

Leads to weight loss, dull coat, diarrhoea. Deworming is essential.

8. Prevention and basic treatment

Daily checklist

  • Observe walking pattern
  • Check appetite
  • Clean hooves
  • Look for swelling or cuts
  • Provide clean water

When to call a vet

  • Horse refuses to eat
  • Severe limping
  • High fever or heavy breathing
  • Signs of colic
  • Deep cuts or bleeding

Never give human medicine to horses. Wrong dosage can be dangerous.

9. Summary

Horse care requires patience, daily observation and gentle handling. A clean stable, balanced diet, regular hoof care and timely veterinary attention keep horses healthy, cooperative and strong. With consistent care and training, horses build deep trust with their caretakers and perform better in daily work, riding or companionship.
